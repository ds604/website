Review Criteria 1: Creativity of the design (25%)

Adapts and applies concepts from outside network security in a compelling and original manner.

Overall Creativity Score

Good

Comments on Creativity

The entry pulls from graphic ideas outside of the domain of network security. While it's not necessarily clear that the results may lend themselves to a usable result when constraints of actual production usage are taken into account, the entry specifically addresses the issue of minimizing visual clutter and providing an overview of overall network health, appropriate to a large display. Notably, it addresses the issue of how users might go about interacting with the display. Though it's not clear that the strategy would work so well, credit given for addressing this issue.

Comments on Scale

The design would have problems scaling to a large number of issues present in a large and changing network. Additional comments in the section below.

Comments on Overall Submission

Comments on guiding metaphor for the design:
The written description describes the motivation for the design being working process, assignment of tasks in a master control room setting. While this is valid, the metaphor appears to get mixed up with the description of the network. The metaphor is not motivated in as compelling a way by activity derived from the domain of network health. It's not clear that analysis of network health lends itself to being displayed in this paradigm in a natural way. The wheel metaphor appears not to capture aspects of hierarchical relationships in a natural way: if wheels need to split apart to show detail, this suggests that the primary metaphor doesn't actually capture the information intended, or that use of space in the display is less than optimal.

It's not clear that abstracting away from geographic distribution gains any compelling advantage. In not being displayed as a map, this is removing an important piece of information, and it's not clear that what's gained from the display justifies the loss.

Issues:
"the radius of a segment is determined by the volume of its issues."
Would regions with no issues disappear? What if certain regions dominate? Can issues that span regions be represented?

Scaling may be problematic: It seems likely that there would be many green circles, and not as many red issues, as are shown in the design presentation. Dot size doesn't appear to capture the information it's intended to: it doesn't seem to make sense that routine issues are bigger than crisis issues. The dot metaphor doesn't seem to convey overall health in a straightforward manner. In looking at the display, we're looking to answer a question something along the lines of, How are we doing? How are things going over here? The operation we're asking for in the language of this design is a summation over dots. But it's not clear how to go about adding up dots.

Auto-layout depending on viewer distance may be problematic. An analyst may in fact want to address issues pertinent to full regions, but the display carries the assumption that this is not the case. Not wanting to see subregions doesn't appear straightforward; but speaking about overall network health is in fact a primary point of what this challenge aims for.

Users may be inclined not to get too close to avoid having the display explode into sub-galaxies. This would affect the ability to perform an effective analysis. A persistent base layout may be necessary for the practical reason of being able to have predictable results when trying to use the display with multiple users. Repositioning another analyst's work by stepping into the work zone would definitely be problematic. It's also not quite clear what the rotation of the "planets" buys in terms of capturing information about the network.

It's not clear that geographic distribution is the primary information sought in determining network health. If other notions of distance or connectivity information are more relevant, the design may need to take into account alternate viewing strategies. It's also not clear how this design would account for issues which span multiple regions. Pre-determined zones that the wheels split into may not in fact be the relevant ones for what analyst wishes to examine.

General comments:
Discrete metaphors -- like the set number of rings around the wheel, the categorization of the issues, and the interaction zones -- allow simplicity, but can be problematic in production usage at the scale of this challenge (e.g. how to determine what category an issue belongs to).

The lack of a temporal domain is notable. It is mentioned that solved issue will disappear. But the history of issues and their development may be an important way to gain information about the state of a crisis. This design element may remove this important indicator.
